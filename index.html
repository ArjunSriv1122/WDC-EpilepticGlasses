E P I L U X

Active shutter glasses designed for photosensitive epilepsy users. They react instantly to sudden lighting changes, helping reduce visual triggers while keeping vision clear.

About Our Device

We created glasses for photosensitive epilepsy patients.

These glasses use specialized LCD Shutter panels to adjust to sudden changes in lighting.

Around 1 in 4,000 people have photosensitive epilepsy (PSE) according to the Epilepsy Foundation, a condition triggered by sudden changes in light such as bright flashes or strobe effects. These sudden changes can cause individuals with PSE to experience extreme headaches, nausea, dizziness, and often incapacitating seizures. While there are many medications available, we wanted to create a practical solution that works in conjunction with medication to prevent dangerous triggers.

Glasses that filter out specific wavelengths of light exist, but they do not address dynamic, unpredictable changes. Epillux bridges this gap by introducing active, real-time protection. Instead of using static lenses that are unfit for certain indoor environments, our shutter lenses provide a barrier that only activates when a threat is detected. This allows our users to participate in new experiences without the cumbersome sight-restriction of polarized lenses or the potential unreliability of medication.

How To Use Our Glasses

  1. Place the glasses comfortably on your face so the lenses fully cover your eyes, with the bridge resting upon your nose.
  2. Power on the glasses.
  3. The glasses will automatically adjust to any triggers, as well as regular sunlight.
  4. When finished using the glasses, remove the frame carefully and set it down on a stable surface, ideally near an outlet.
  5. When rested safely, plug in the LiPo battery charger to ensure maximum uptime for next use.

How They Work & Our Sustainability Plan

Coded in C++, our camera is linked to our controller via onboard WIFI provided by our ESP32. The camera feed is formatted and displayed on a web link, where we create a “mask” by tuning the exposure and brightness extremely low while increasing the contrast. This gives us a black on white mask → the 2 states our program is monitoring. As light changes, these pixels that illuminate or dim will change states. When the camera detects approximately 2% of its pixels have changed state, the glasses will activate, with a response time of 55 milliseconds. The camera is placed at the center of the frame, providing a full vision of what the wearer would see, essentially becoming one’s third eye. We decided to choose a camera over other sensors, such as photodiodes and light sensors, because the camera gave us much more freedom and viability. We previously tested photodiodes and light sensors (BH1750), which all had one main problem: they could not accurately detect changes from afar; an issue that is fixed by the increased visibility of the camera acting as our sensor. Additionally, our glasses integrate the features of a normal pair of sunglasses by dimming automatically in bright environments.

In the future, we plan to implement solar panels along our frame that are routed to our rechargeable LiPo batteries. This would not only serve to greatly extend battery life, but may also serve as the main power source for our glasses if proven to be especially effective. This is a practical addition to our glasses due to their functionality as sunglasses. The user would ideally be able to store charge for indoor environments while they are using the glasses in bright outdoor settings, creating a self-sustaining cycle of protection. By utilizing thin-film solar cells on the edges of the frame, the device can passively harvest energy during peak daylight. This reduces the need for manual charging and guarantees that our glasses remain functional in critical times.
